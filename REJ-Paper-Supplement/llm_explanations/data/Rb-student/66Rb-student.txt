#### 3. **Rebuilding Meaning from Bottom Up**  
   - **Atomic propositions:**  
     - \( a \): "The system is in a state where property \( a \) holds."  
     - \( b \): "The system is in a state where property \( b \) holds."  
   - **Subformula \( F(a) \):**  
     "At some future time (including now), \( a \) **must become true**."  
     *Constraints:* At least one state in the timeline must satisfy \( a \).  
   - **Subformula \( F(b) \):**  
     "At some future time (including now), \( b \) **must become true**."  
     *Constraints:* At least one state in the timeline must satisfy \( b \).  
   - **Conjunction \( F(a) \land F(b) \):**  
     "Both \( F(a) \) and \( F(b) \) must hold" â†’  
     "*Eventually, \( a \) becomes true, **and** eventually, \( b \) becomes true.*"  
     *Constraints:*  
     - \( a \) must occur at least once.  
     - \( b \) must occur at least once.  
     - Timing is independent:  
       - \( a \) and \( b \) can occur **in the same state** or **in different states**.  
       - No constraints on order (e.g., \( a \) could happen before \( b \), after \( b \), or simultaneously).  

#### 4. **Refined Natural Language Phrasing**  
   > "**Both \( a \) and \( b \) will eventually become true, at any time.**  
   > This means:  
   > - The system must reach a state where \( a \) holds (at least once).  
   > - The system must reach a state where \( b \) holds (at least once).  
   > - These states may be the same or different, and no specific order is required."  

### **Key Implications**  
- If the formula is **satisfied**:  
  - There exists a state \( s_i \) (with \( i \geq 0 \)) where \( a \) is true.  
  - There exists a state \( s_j \) (with \( j \geq 0 \)) where \( b \) is true.  
  - \( i \) and \( j \) can be identical or distinct.  
- If the formula is **violated**:  
  - Either \( a \) **never** holds at any point in the future, or \( b \) **never** holds.  
  - Example violation: A timeline where \( a \) is always absent (even if \( b \) occurs) â†’ violates \( F(a) \) and thus the whole formula.  

### **Why This Matters in Requirements Engineering**  
This formula specifies **minimal guarantees** about system behavior:  
- It ensures that \( a \) and \( b \) must each happen *at least once* but imposes **no deadlines, ordering, or recurrence constraints**.  
- Example requirement:  
  > "The system must eventually log a report (\( a \)) and eventually notify the user (\( b \))."  
  > (Logging and notification can happen independently or together.)
