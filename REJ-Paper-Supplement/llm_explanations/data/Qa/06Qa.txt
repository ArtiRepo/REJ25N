- **Original LTL formula**:  
  $ X \neg b \land d $

- **Breakdown (parsing tree)**:  
  - The top-level operator is **conjunction** ($ \land $), combining two subformulas:  
    1. $ X \neg b $ (the "next" operator applied to $ \neg b $).  
    2. $ d $ (an atomic proposition).  
  - $ X \neg b $ breaks down into:  
    - **Next** ($ X $) operator applied to $ \neg b $.  
    - $ \neg b $ is the negation of proposition $ b $.  
  - $ d $ is a base proposition.  

- **Rebuild meaning from the bottom of the tree up**:  
  1. $ \neg b $: "At the next time step, $ b $ is false."  
  2. $ X \neg b $: "In the next time step, $ b $ is false."  
  3. $ d $: "At the current time step, $ d $ is true."  
  4. $ X \neg b \land d $: "In the next time step, $ b $ is false **and** at the current time step, $ d $ is true."  

- **Refine natural language phrasing for clarity**:  
  "At the current time step, $ d $ must be true, and in the immediately following time step, $ b $ must be false."  

  **Explanation**:  
  - The formula imposes constraints on **two consecutive time steps**:  
    - **Current time**: $ d $ is true.  
    - **Next time**: $ b $ is false.  
  - It does **not** require $ d $ to hold in the future or $ b $ to be false at all future times. Instead, it enforces this specific pairing of conditions for the **immediate next step** and the **current step**.  
  - This is a **point-wise constraint** (applies to a single moment in the system's behavior) rather than a global or ongoing requirement.
