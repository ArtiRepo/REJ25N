- **Original LTL formula**:  
  $ t \Rightarrow (x \land y) $  

- **Breakdown into a parsing tree**:  
  - Root: Implication ($ \Rightarrow $)  
    - Left child: Proposition $ t $  
    - Right child: Conjunction ($ \land $)  
      - Left child: Proposition $ x $  
      - Right child: Proposition $ y $  

- **Rebuild meaning from the bottom of the tree up**:  
  1. The conjunction $ x \land y $ requires both $ x $ and $ y $ to be true *at the same time*.  
  2. The implication $ t \Rightarrow (x \land y) $ enforces that if $ t $ is true, then $ x $ and $ y $ must *simultaneously* be true.  
  3. This means that $ t $ cannot occur in any state or moment where $ x $ or $ y $ is false. The formula imposes a safety constraint: **whenever $ t $ is true, $ x $ and $ y $ must also be true in the same state**.  

- **Refined natural language phrasing**:  
  "If $ t $ is true, then both $ x $ and $ y $ must be true at the same time."  
  **Explanation**:  
  - The formula is satisfied when, in every possible scenario or state of the system, $ t $ never holds alone or with only one of $ x $ or $ y $.  
  - It imposes a constraint on the system: **$ t $ can only occur when $ x $ and $ y $ are both true simultaneously**.  
  - This is a *state-level* requirement (assuming $ t, x, y $ are atomic propositions) rather than a time-dependent one, but in LTL, it applies to all time points in the system's execution (i.e., "at every moment in time").  

**Final Translation**:  
"At every time point, if $ t $ is true, then $ x $ and $ y $ must both be true simultaneously."
