### Translation of the LTL Formula: \( X  t \implies X  y \)

#### Step 1: Original LTL Formula  
\( X  t \implies X  y \)  

#### Step 2: Parsing Tree Breakdown  
The formula is an **implication** (\(\implies\)) with two subformulas:  
- **Left operand**: \( X  t \) ("next \( t \)")  
- **Right operand**: \( X  y \) ("next \( y \)")  

**Parsing tree**:  
```  
    IMPLIES  
    /      \  
  X(t)     X(y)  
   |        |  
   t        y  
```  

#### Step 3: Rebuild Meaning Bottom-Up  
Start with the leaf propositions and apply operators:  

1. **Atomic proposition \( t \)**  
   Statement: "\( t \) must be true in the next state" (under \( X \)).  

2. **Atomic proposition \( y \)**  
   Statement: "\( y \) must be true in the next state" (under \( X \)).  

3. **Operator \( X(t) \)**:  
   "In the **immediately next state of the execution**, \( t \) must hold."  

4. **Operator \( X(y) \)**:  
   "In the **immediately next state of the execution**, \( y \) must hold."  

5. **Implication \( \implies \)** (combining \( X(t) \) and \( X(y) \)):  
   The entire formula \( X  t \implies X  y \) means:  
   - **If \( X  t \) is true** (i.e., \( t \) holds in the next state),  
   - **then \( X  y \) must be true** (i.e., \( y \) must hold in the next state).  
   - **If \( X  t \) is false** (i.e., \( t \) is **false** in the next state),  
   - then the implication is **automatically satisfied** (no constraint on \( y \)).  

#### Step 4: Natural Language Phrasing & Explanation  
**Refined translation**:  
> **"If \( t \) is true in the immediate next state, then \( y \) must also be true in that same next state."**  

**Explanation of constraints over time**:  
- This formula **only constrains the very next state** (one step into the future).  
- **When the formula is satisfied**:  
  - If \( t \) holds in the next state, \( y \) must also hold in that state.  
  - If \( t \) **does not hold** in the next state, \( y \) can be **either true or false** (no constraint).  
- **Key temporal behavior**:  
  - The system is **forbidden** from transitioning into a state where \( t \) is true but \( y \) is false.  
  - In all other cases (e.g., \( t \) true/\( y \) true, \( t \) false/\( y \) true, \( t \) false/\( y \) false), the formula holds.  
- **Beyond the next state**: The formula does **not constrain any other future states**.
