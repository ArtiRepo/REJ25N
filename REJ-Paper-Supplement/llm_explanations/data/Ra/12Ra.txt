#### Step 3: Rebuilding Meaning from the Bottom Up  
We start from the leaves and move upward:  

1. **Base atomic propositions**:  
   - \( a \): Represents a system condition (e.g., "event \( a \) occurs"). True if \( a \) holds at a given state.  
   - \( d \): Similar to \( a \), represents a distinct condition (e.g., "event \( d \) occurs").  

2. **Subformula \( a \lor d \) (OR operator)**:  
   - True if **either \( a \) is true, \( d \) is true, or both** at the same state.  
   - Example: A system state satisfies \( a \lor d \) if "\( a \) happens" OR "\( d \) happens".  

3. **Root formula \( b \, \text{U} \, (a \lor d) \) (Until operator)**:  
   - Semantics:  
     - There exists a **future state \( j \)** where \( (a \lor d) \) is true.  
     - **From the current state up to (but excluding) \( j \)**, \( b \) must be true at every state.  
   - Key details:  
     - **Eventuality**: The OR condition (\( a \lor d \)) **must eventually become true** (liveness).  
     - **Continuity constraint**: \( b \) must hold **uninterruptedly** until the instant before \( a \lor d \) becomes true. If \( a \lor d \) holds immediately (current state), \( b \) is unconstrained.  
   - Violation condition:  
     - Fails if \( a \lor d \) never becomes true, OR if \( b \) is false in any state before the first occurrence of \( a \lor d \).  

#### Step 4: Refined Natural Language Phrasing  
To ensure clarity and completeness:  
- Explicitly separate the eventuality and continuity requirements.  
- Avoid ambiguity about the timing of \( b \) and \( (a \lor d) \).  

**Final natural language explanation**:  
> "*\( b \) must continuously hold until the moment immediately before \( a \) or \( d \) becomes true. Moreover, \( a \) or \( d \) must eventually become true in the future.*"  

**Key implications**:  
- **Behavior over time**:  
  - \( b \) is temporarily constrained (e.g., a safety property) but is **released** as soon as \( a \) or \( d \) occurs.  
  - The system **cannot avoid** triggering \( a \) or \( d \) forever.  
- **Boundary cases**:  
  - If \( a \) or \( d \) is true **now**, the formula holds regardless of \( b \) (no prior states to enforce \( b \)).  
  - If \( b \) is false before \( a \lor d \), the formula fails even if \( a \lor d \) later occurs.  

**Example context**:  
Suppose \( b \)= "system is in startup mode", \( a \)= "operation begins", \( d \)= "error occurs". The formula means:  
> "*The system must stay in startup mode until either normal operation starts or an error is detected. One of these events must eventually happen.*"
