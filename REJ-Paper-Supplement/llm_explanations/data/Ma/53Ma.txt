### **Original LTL Formula:**
**F*a* → G*b***

---

### **Parsing Tree Breakdown:**
The formula is an **implication (→)** with two subformulas:
1. **Left subformula (antecedent):** *F*a*
   - *F* is the **"eventually"** (future) operator.
   - *a* is an atomic proposition (a condition that can be true or false at any point in time).
   - So, *F*a* means: *"a will eventually become true (now or at some future point)."*

2. **Right subformula (consequent):** *G*b*
   - *G* is the **"globally"** (always in the future) operator.
   - *b* is another atomic proposition.
   - So, *G*b* means: *"b must remain true at all future points from now onward."*

The full formula combines these as an implication:
*"If (F*a*), then (G*b*)."*

---

### **Rebuilding Meaning from the Bottom Up:**
1. **Antecedent (*F*a*):**
   - The system must reach a state where *a* holds at least once in the future (including the present).
   - This does *not* require *a* to stay true forever—just that it happens at some point.

2. **Consequent (*G*b*):**
   - Once the implication is triggered (i.e., *F*a* is satisfied), *b* must hold **continuously from that point onward**.
   - This is a **permanent constraint**: *b* can never become false again after *a* is first observed.

3. **Implication (→):**
   - The consequent (*G*b*) is only enforced **if** the antecedent (*F*a*) is satisfied.
   - If *a* never becomes true, the implication is **vacuously satisfied** (no constraint on *b*).
   - If *a* does become true at some point, then from that moment, *b* must hold forever.

---

### **Refined Natural Language Translation:**
**"If *a* eventually occurs at some point in the future, then from that point onward, *b* must always remain true."**

#### **Key Clarifications:**
- **When is the formula satisfied?**
  - If *a* never happens, the formula is trivially satisfied (no requirements on *b*).
  - If *a* happens at time *t*, then for all times *t’ ≥ t*, *b* must hold.

- **When is the formula violated?**
  - If *a* occurs at some point, and later *b* becomes false (even once), the formula is violated.
  - Example violation:
    - At time 3: *a* becomes true (satisfying *F*a*).
    - At time 5: *b* becomes false → violates *G*b*.

- **Temporal Dependence:**
  - The constraint on *b* is **activated** by the first occurrence of *a*.
  - Before *a* occurs, *b* can fluctuate freely.

---
### **Alternative Phrasings (for context):**
- *"Once *a* happens, *b* must stay true forever after."*
- *"The first occurrence of *a* locks *b* into a permanently true state."*
- *"*b* is invariant after *a*’s first appearance."*

This captures the **triggered invariant** nature of the formula.
