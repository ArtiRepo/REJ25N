### **Original LTL Formula:**
**(a U b) ∧ G(a)**

---

### **Parsing Tree Breakdown:**
The formula consists of two main subformulas connected by a conjunction (∧):

1. **(a U b)** – *"a until b"*
   - **a**: A proposition that must hold at the current and intermediate states.
   - **b**: A proposition that must eventually hold in the future.
   - **U (Until)**: Requires that **a** remains true *at least* until **b** becomes true at some future point.

2. **G(a)** – *"Globally a"*
   - **G (Globally)**: Requires that **a** holds *at all points in time* (now and forever).

The entire formula is the conjunction (∧) of these two subformulas.

---

### **Rebuilding Meaning from the Bottom Up:**

1. **G(a) ("Globally a")**
   - This means **a** must be true *at every moment in time*, with no exceptions.
   - If **a** ever becomes false, the formula fails.

2. **(a U b) ("a until b")**
   - This means **a** must hold *continuously* from now until some future point where **b** becomes true.
   - **b** *must* eventually happen (otherwise, the "until" condition is never satisfied).
   - Once **b** becomes true, the "until" condition is satisfied, and **a** no longer needs to hold afterward.

3. **Conjunction: (a U b) ∧ G(a)**
   - **G(a)** enforces that **a** must *always* be true.
   - **(a U b)** enforces that **a** must hold *until* **b** occurs.
   - However, since **G(a)** requires **a** to hold *forever*, the "until" condition imposes an *additional* constraint: **b** must eventually happen (because if it didn’t, **a U b** would fail, even if **a** stayed true forever).
   - But if **b** *does* happen, **G(a)** still requires **a** to remain true *even after* **b** occurs.

   **Key Insight:**
   - **G(a)** alone would allow **b** to never happen (since **a** is always true).
   - **(a U b)** alone would allow **a** to become false *after* **b** happens.
   - The conjunction **forces both**:
     - **a** must always be true (**G(a)**).
     - **b** must eventually happen (**a U b** implies **F(b)**, "eventually b").
     - Even after **b** occurs, **a** must still hold (due to **G(a)**).

---

### **Refined Natural Language Translation:**
*"**a** must always hold true, and at some future point, **b** must occur (while **a** remains true both before and after **b** happens)."*

#### **Alternative Phrasings (for clarity):**
- *"**a** is permanently true, and **b** eventually happens (without **a** ever failing)."*
- *"**a** is an invariant, and **b** is guaranteed to occur at some point, but **a** must still hold afterward."*
- *"**a** must be true forever, and **b** must eventually become true (with **a** never becoming false)."*

---

### **When is the Formula Satisfied?**
The formula is satisfied by a system trace if:
1. **a** is true in *every state* (now and forever).
2. There exists *at least one future state* where **b** becomes true.
3. **a** does *not* become false at any point (even after **b** occurs).

#### **When is the Formula Violated?**
The formula fails if:
- **a** becomes false at *any* point (violating **G(a)**), *or*
- **b** *never* becomes true (violating **(a U b)**).

---
### **Example Scenarios:**
| Trace (sequence of states) | Satisfies **(a U b) ∧ G(a)**? | Reason |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------|--------|
| a=true, a=true, ..., b=true, a=true, ... | ✅ Yes | **a** always holds, and **b** eventually occurs. |
| a=true, a=true, a=true, ... (b never true) | ❌ No | **b** never happens (violates **a U b**). |
| a=true, a=false, b=true, ... | ❌ No | **a** becomes false (violates **G(a)**). |
| a=true, b=true, a=false, ... | ❌ No | **a** becomes false after **b** (violates **G(a)**). |

---
### **Key Takeaways:**
- **G(a)** is the stricter condition: it requires **a** to hold *forever*.
- **(a U b)** adds the requirement that **b** must happen *at some point*.
- The conjunction means **b** is *guaranteed* to occur, but **a** must still hold *before, during, and after* **b**.

This formula is useful for specifying systems where:
- A safety property (**a**) must *always* hold (e.g., "the system is never in an unsafe state").
- A liveness property (**b**) must *eventually* occur (e.g., "the system eventually reaches a goal state"), but the safety property must persist even after the goal is reached.
